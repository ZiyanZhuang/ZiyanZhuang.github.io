Some ideas

My one idea is driven by the recognition that while natural enzymes are exquisite catalysts, their inherent limitations in scope and stability necessitate the development of artificial counterparts. Overcoming the current bottlenecks in enzyme engineering â€“ moving beyond empirical methods like directed evolution to predictable, de novo design â€“ requires a deep understanding of the fundamental physical and chemical principles governing enzyme catalysis.

Towards this goal, I am particularly interested in leveraging multiscale simulation techniques rooted in first-principles quantum mechanics and statistical mechanics. My research ideas revolve around developing and applying computational frameworks that begin with the intrinsic quantum chemical nature of a target reaction's transition state. By precisely characterizing the electronic structure and geometry of this transient state, we can define the ideal catalytic microenvironment required within a protein scaffold. Subsequently, techniques like Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling and energy optimization algorithms can be employed to search the vast sequence and conformational space for amino acid combinations capable of stabilizing this transition state.

This computational design phase is complemented by molecular dynamics (MD) simulations, which provide crucial insights into the dynamic behavior, flexibility, and solvent effects of the designed enzyme-substrate complexes under different thermodynamic ensembles. Calculating key thermodynamic quantities, such as binding and activation free energies, using enhanced sampling methods allows for a theoretical evaluation of catalytic efficiency. Finally, high-accuracy quantum mechanics/molecular mechanics (QM/MM) methods are indispensable for refining the catalytic mechanism and validating the design at an atomic level.
